What Is a DEXA Scan?

A DEXA scan (short for dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry) is a medical imaging technology that precisely measures body composition by distinguishing between fat tissue, lean muscle mass, and bone mineral density. Originally developed to diagnose osteoporosis, DEXA has become the clinical gold standard for detailed body composition analysis [1].

Unlike a bathroom scale or BMI calculation, a DEXA scan shows you where fat and muscle are distributed across your body. It breaks things down by region (arms, legs, trunk, and the android/gynoid zones), giving you a level of detail that no other widely available method can match.

For men pursuing testosterone replacement therapy (TRT) or other hormone optimization protocols, DEXA provides objective, trackable data that validates whether treatment is producing real changes in lean mass and fat distribution. It removes guesswork and gives both patient and physician a clear picture of progress.

What DEXA Measures

A full-body DEXA scan produces detailed measurements across several categories. Understanding what each metric means helps you get the most from your results.

Total Body Fat Percentage

Your overall ratio of fat tissue to total body weight. DEXA measures this with approximately 1–2% precision, far more accurate than bioelectrical impedance scales or skinfold calipers. For adult men, 10–20% body fat is generally considered a healthy athletic range, while 20–25% is average.

Regional Fat Distribution

DEXA reports fat mass separately for each limb and the trunk, including the critical android (abdominal) and gynoid (hip/thigh) regions. The android-to-gynoid ratio is a key marker for metabolic risk; higher abdominal fat is strongly correlated with cardiovascular disease, insulin resistance, and type 2 diabetes [4].

Lean Muscle Mass

Total and regional lean tissue mass, including skeletal muscle, organs, and water content. This is one of the most important metrics for men on TRT, as testosterone directly promotes protein synthesis and muscle growth [2]. DEXA can detect lean mass gains as small as 0.5 kg between scans.

Bone Mineral Density (BMD)

DEXA measures bone density at the spine, hip, and whole body, expressed as a T-score compared to a healthy young adult reference. Low testosterone is a known risk factor for reduced bone density, making BMD tracking especially relevant for men considering or undergoing hormone therapy [3].

Visceral Adipose Tissue (VAT)

Many modern DEXA scanners estimate visceral fat, the deep abdominal fat surrounding internal organs. Visceral fat is metabolically active and produces inflammatory cytokines linked to heart disease, diabetes, and hormonal disruption. Tracking VAT over time provides insight into metabolic health improvements that the scale alone cannot capture.

Appendicular Lean Mass Index (ALMI)

ALMI is calculated from the lean mass in your arms and legs, divided by your height squared. It's the primary clinical measure used to diagnose sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss). Men with low testosterone are at elevated risk for sarcopenia, making ALMI a valuable tracking metric during hormone optimization.

How a DEXA Scan Works

DEXA technology works by passing two low-dose X-ray beams at different energy levels through the body. Different tissues (fat, lean mass, and bone) absorb these beams at different rates. A detector on the other side measures the remaining energy, and software calculates the precise composition of each pixel.

The scan itself is straightforward and non-invasive:

  1. Check in. You arrive at the facility and remove any metal objects (belt buckles, jewelry, zippers). Most facilities allow you to wear light athletic clothing.
  2. Lie flat. You lie on your back on an open, padded table: there's no enclosed tube like an MRI.
  3. Stay still. A mechanical arm passes slowly over your body from head to toe. The process takes 10–20 minutes depending on the scanner model.
  4. Get results. Many facilities provide a printed report immediately or within minutes. The report includes total and regional body composition data, bone density scores, and comparison charts.

The radiation exposure from a single DEXA scan is approximately 0.001 mSv, roughly equivalent to a few hours of natural background radiation and significantly less than a chest X-ray (0.1 mSv) or a cross-country flight (0.03 mSv) [6].

How to Prepare for Your DEXA Scan

While a DEXA scan requires no special preparation, consistency between scans is critical for accurate comparisons over time. Following these guidelines ensures your results are reliable and reproducible.

1

Hydrate normally

Drink your usual amount of water. Excessive hydration or dehydration can shift lean mass readings by 1–2 lbs. Try to match your hydration level between scans.

2

Avoid large meals

Do not eat a large meal within 2 hours of your scan. Food in the stomach and intestines registers as lean mass and can skew results.

3

Skip calcium supplements

Avoid calcium supplements for 24 hours before your scan. Calcium in the digestive tract can interfere with bone density measurements.

4

Avoid exercise beforehand

Do not exercise intensely in the 12 hours before your scan. Exercise causes temporary fluid shifts between tissues that can affect readings.

5

Wear consistent clothing

Wear light athletic clothing without metal (zippers, underwire, buttons). Wear the same type of clothing each time you scan.

6

Scan at the same time of day

Body weight fluctuates throughout the day. Schedule scans at the same time, ideally morning, for the most consistent comparisons.

Reading Your DEXA Results

A DEXA report can feel dense on first read, but the key metrics are straightforward once you know what to look for. Here's how to interpret the most important numbers.

Body Fat Percentage

Your total body fat percentage is the headline number most people look at first. For men, general ranges are:

CategoryBody Fat %
Essential Fat2–5%
Athletic6–13%
Fitness14–17%
Average18–24%
Obese25%+

Android/Gynoid Ratio

This ratio compares abdominal fat to hip/thigh fat. A ratio above 1.0 indicates higher abdominal fat distribution, which is associated with greater metabolic risk. Men undergoing TRT often see meaningful improvements in this ratio as testosterone promotes visceral fat reduction [2].

T-Score (Bone Density)

Your T-score compares your bone density to a healthy 30-year-old reference. A T-score of -1.0 or above is normal. Between -1.0 and -2.5 indicates osteopenia (low bone mass), and below -2.5 indicates osteoporosis. Low testosterone is directly linked to reduced bone mineral density, making this a critical metric for men with hypogonadism.

Lean Mass Asymmetry

DEXA reports lean mass for each limb separately. A difference greater than 10% between left and right sides may indicate a muscle imbalance that could increase injury risk. This information is valuable for tailoring training programs, particularly for men using TRT to rebuild muscle mass after a period of low testosterone.

DEXA Scans and TRT Monitoring

Testosterone replacement therapy is designed to restore hormonal balance, and one of the most tangible outcomes patients expect is improved body composition: more muscle, less fat. DEXA scanning provides the objective data needed to verify these outcomes and guide treatment adjustments.

Why DEXA Matters for TRT Patients

Clinical trials have demonstrated that testosterone therapy in hypogonadal men produces measurable increases in lean body mass (averaging 3–5 lbs in the first year) and decreases in fat mass, particularly visceral abdominal fat [5]. However, the scale alone is a poor indicator of these changes because muscle gain and fat loss can offset each other: a patient may weigh the same while their body composition has improved significantly.

DEXA resolves this by independently measuring lean mass and fat mass. A patient whose weight stayed the same but gained 4 lbs of muscle and lost 4 lbs of fat has made substantial progress, progress that only DEXA can objectively document.

How Physicians Use DEXA Data

Board-certified physicians managing TRT protocols use DEXA data alongside full lab panels to evaluate treatment effectiveness. For patients targeting visceral fat, a DEXA scan pairs naturally with peptides for visceral belly fat. DEXA results help answer critical questions:

  • Is the patient gaining lean mass at an expected rate?
  • Is visceral fat decreasing, reducing metabolic risk?
  • Are bone density levels stable or improving?
  • Does the dosage need adjustment based on body composition response?

This data-driven approach ensures that testosterone replacement therapy is producing real, measurable results, not just subjective improvements in how a patient feels.

Baseline and Follow-Up Protocol

The recommended DEXA monitoring schedule for TRT patients typically follows this pattern:

Baseline

Before starting TRT: establishes your starting body composition for future comparison.

3–6 Months

First follow-up to assess initial response. Most patients show measurable lean mass increases and fat reduction by this point.

12 Months

Annual comparison to evaluate long-term progress, bone density changes, and treatment optimization.

Annually thereafter

Ongoing monitoring to maintain progress, catch early bone density changes, and adjust protocols as needed.

How Often Should You Get a DEXA Scan?

The optimal scanning frequency depends on your goals and current health protocols:

  • General health monitoring: Once per year is sufficient for most adults to track age-related changes in muscle and bone density.
  • Active body recomposition: Every 3–6 months during aggressive fat loss or muscle-building phases. Scanning more frequently than every 3 months is unlikely to show meaningful changes and wastes resources.
  • TRT monitoring: Baseline scan before starting therapy, follow-up at 3–6 months, then annually. Your prescribing physician may recommend more frequent scans if body composition changes are slower than expected.
  • Osteoporosis risk: Every 1–2 years for bone density specifically, as recommended by the International Society for Clinical Densitometry [3].

For the most accurate trend data, use the same scanner and facility each time. Different DEXA machines can produce slightly different readings due to calibration differences, which introduces noise into your longitudinal data [6].
