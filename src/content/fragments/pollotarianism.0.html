TL;DR

Pollotarianism is a semi-vegetarian diet where poultry is the only meat you eat. Chicken, turkey, and duck stay on the menu; beef, pork, lamb, and (in the strict version) fish come off it. The rest of the plate is built from vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, nuts, and optionally eggs and dairy. The evidence behind the core move is solid: swapping one daily serving of red meat for poultry was associated with a 19% lower risk of coronary heart disease in a 26-year study of over 84,000 women.[1] It's flexible, affordable, and far easier to sustain than most named diets. The catch: how you prepare the poultry matters as much as the swap itself.

What Is Pollotarianism?

A pollotarian is someone who eats poultry but no other meat. The word comes from pollo, the Spanish and Italian word for chicken, bolted onto the same suffix that gives us "vegetarian." Pronounce it "pull-oh-TAIR-ee-un." If you've heard the terms "pollo-vegetarian" or "chicketarian," they describe the same eating pattern.

Pollotarianism sits in the semi-vegetarian family alongside pescatarianism (fish but no other meat) and flexitarianism (mostly plants, occasional anything). The rules are simple. Chicken, turkey, duck, and other poultry are allowed. Beef, pork, lamb, veal, goat, and game meats are out. Strict pollotarians also skip fish and seafood; those who keep fish in the rotation follow what's called a pesce-pollotarian diet. Eggs and dairy are a personal call — most pollotarians eat both, but neither is required.

There's no founder, no book to buy, and no phase system. Pollotarianism is a boundary, not a protocol: one line drawn around which animal foods you eat, with everything else — calories, macros, meal timing — left up to you. That flexibility is exactly why it works for people who want most of the benefits of a plant-forward diet without giving up meat entirely, and why researchers group it with the semi-vegetarian patterns that consistently show better health outcomes than standard Western eating.[6]

Why People Go Pollotarian

Four motivations show up again and again, and they stack — most pollotarians are driven by more than one.

  • •Health: Red and processed meat carry documented associations with heart disease, colorectal cancer, and earlier mortality. The World Health Organization's cancer agency classifies processed meat as a Group 1 carcinogen and red meat as probably carcinogenic. Poultry carries none of those classifications.[3]
  • •Environment: Producing beef emits roughly 10 times more greenhouse gas per gram of protein than producing chicken, and uses dramatically more land and water. Dropping red meat is one of the highest-impact dietary changes an individual can make.[9]
  • •Budget: Chicken and turkey are consistently among the cheapest animal proteins per gram. A pollotarian grocery bill built on poultry, beans, grains, and seasonal produce usually undercuts a red-meat-heavy one.
  • •A stepping stone: Many people use pollotarianism as a transition toward vegetarianism — or land here permanently after finding full vegetarianism too restrictive. Because it's a boundary rather than a program, there's nothing to "fall off."

Adherence and whole-body health lead the scorecard. There's no calorie counting, no banned macronutrient, and no food you have to weigh — you're removing a category, not managing a system, which is why people stay pollotarian for years rather than weeks. Weight loss scores a 6 because the diet creates no automatic deficit; it makes a lean, high-satiety plate easier to build but won't do the work for you. Evidence strength sits at 5 for an honest reason: almost no studies test pollotarianism by name. The score rests on strong substitution research (red meat out, poultry in) and semi-vegetarian cohort data rather than trials of the diet itself.[1]

Macro & Nutrition Profile

Pollotarianism doesn't prescribe macros, so the profile below reflects a typical well-built pollotarian day rather than a rulebook. It lands close to what mainstream dietary guidelines recommend — which is part of the appeal.

Protein25%

Fiber: 25–38g/day
Meals: 3 meals + snacks

Protein is the easy part. A 3.5 oz (100g) serving of skinless chicken breast delivers about 31g of complete protein with roughly 3.6g of fat, making it one of the leanest protein sources available — and poultry supplies B12, niacin, B6, selenium, zinc, and iron along with it.[8] At 25% of calories, protein sits squarely in the range that weight-loss research supports for satiety and muscle preservation.

The fiber target deserves equal attention. Because pollotarianism pushes plants to the center of the plate, hitting 25g or more of daily fiber is realistic — and the payoff is large. A Lancet meta-analysis spanning 185 prospective studies found the highest fiber intakes were associated with 15-30% reductions in all-cause and cardiovascular mortality, with the greatest benefits between 25g and 29g per day.[10] That's a number most Americans miss by half.

Pollotarian Food List: What You Can Eat

The allowed list is long — this is a diet defined by what it removes, not what it restricts you to. Poultry is the only meat, but everything from the plant kingdom is fair game, and eggs and dairy are optional additions most pollotarians keep.
