What Peptides Do for Skin

Peptides are short chains of amino acids that act as signaling molecules — they tell skin cells to do things like build collagen or remodel the supporting matrix. Unlike a moisturizer that sits on the surface, a signaling peptide aims to influence the skin’s own repair machinery.

In practice, the skin peptide with the deepest research history is GHK-Cu, a copper-binding tripeptide. Separately, collagen peptides are taken orally to supply the building blocks and signals for connective tissue. The two are often confused but work very differently.

GHK-Cu: The Copper Peptide for Skin

GHK-Cu (copper tripeptide-1) occurs naturally in human plasma, where levels are highest in youth and decline with age. It binds copper with high affinity and carries this essential cofactor into tissue, where it stimulates fibroblasts to produce collagen, elastin, and proteoglycans.[1]

It also helps regulate the balance of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and their inhibitors — the enzymes that remodel the skin’s scaffold during repair — and supports angiogenesis and antioxidant pathways involved in tissue regeneration.[2]

  • •Firmness and fine lines — the most common reason people reach for copper peptides
  • •Collagen and elastin support as natural peptide levels fall with age
  • •Wound healing and post-procedure skin repair
  • •Overall skin quality and tone with consistent topical use over weeks to months

What the Evidence Shows

GHK-Cu has a longer research record than most peptides in this space. In-vitro and small human studies of topical formulations report improvements in firmness, elasticity, and appearance, with clear effects on collagen synthesis in cultured cells.[1][3]

We’re candid about the distinction that matters most: the strongest evidence is for topical cosmetic use. Data on injectable or systemic GHK-Cu in people are limited, so compounded injectable use rests more on mechanism and safety than on definitive human efficacy trials.

A realistic expectation: peptides support skin quality gradually, over months of consistent use. They are a complement to — not a replacement for — sun protection, which remains the single most effective anti-aging step.

Collagen & Other Skin-Relevant Peptides

Beyond copper peptides, a few others come up in skin conversations:

  • •Collagen peptides — oral hydrolyzed collagen studied for skin hydration and elasticity; a different mechanism from topical GHK-Cu.
  • •GH secretagogues — act on the growth-hormone axis and are discussed for whole-body aging rather than as targeted skin treatments.
  • •Cosmetic “matrikine” peptides (e.g. Matrixyl-type) — common in over-the-counter serums, with modest supporting data.

How Strong Health Approaches Skin Peptides

Most GHK-Cu use is topical and available over the counter — no prescription needed. Where a compounded or higher-strength preparation is appropriate, it is prescribed only after a physician reviews your skin goals and history, and dispensed through licensed 503A/503B compounding pharmacies.

We treat peptides as one part of a skin plan built on fundamentals: daily sun protection, a proven retinoid where appropriate, and realistic timelines. We’re direct about where evidence is strongest so you spend effort on what works.

Safety & Side Effects

GHK-Cu has a long record in topical cosmetics with a favorable safety profile; reactions are usually limited to mild local irritation, redness, or dryness. Because it carries copper, people with copper sensitivity or a copper-metabolism disorder such as Wilson’s disease should avoid non-cosmetic use.

Peptides discussed here are not FDA-approved drugs; in the U.S. they are dispensed only as pharmacy-compounded preparations from licensed 503A/503B pharmacies under physician supervision. This page is educational and is not medical advice or a dosing guide.
