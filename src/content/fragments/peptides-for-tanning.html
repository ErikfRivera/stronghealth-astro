What Tanning Peptides Are

“Tanning peptides” refers to melanocortin peptides — synthetic analogs of alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone (α-MSH) that stimulate the skin to produce melanin. There are two very different things sold under this umbrella, and conflating them is dangerous:

  • •Melanotan II — an unapproved, unregulated peptide widely sold online and via gray-market channels for cosmetic tanning. It is illegal to sell for human use in many countries and is not quality-controlled.
  • •Afamelanotide (SCENESSE) — a related, FDA-approved medication for a narrow, rare medical condition, administered by specialists.
Strong Health does not provide Melanotan II or any peptide for cosmetic tanning. This page exists to give you the facts, because the risks here are real and often understated by sellers.

How Melanocortin Peptides Work

Melanin is your skin’s natural pigment and its built-in defense against UV damage. Melanocortin peptides activate the melanocortin-1 receptor (MC1R) on pigment cells, pushing them to produce more eumelanin — which darkens the skin. Some of these peptides also act on other melanocortin receptors, which is why side effects can extend well beyond pigmentation (for example, effects on appetite, arousal, and blood pressure).[1]

Crucially, a peptide-induced tan does not make UV exposure safe, and stimulating pigment cells pharmacologically raises distinct concerns of its own.

Melanotan II: The Serious Concerns

Melanotan II is the peptide most people mean by “tanning injections.” The concerns are substantial and well documented in the dermatology literature:

  • •Changes in moles — new, darkening, or atypical melanocytic naevi have been reported in users, and case reports have described melanoma diagnosed in the setting of Melanotan use.[3]
  • •It is unregulated: products sold online have no guarantee of identity, purity, sterility, or dose, and are often self-injected.
  • •Systemic side effects — nausea, facial flushing, spontaneous erections, and blood-pressure changes are commonly reported.
  • •It provides no protection from UV damage and may encourage more sun or sunbed exposure.

Regulatory agencies including the U.S. FDA and the UK’s MHRA have warned against Melanotan products. For any concern about changing moles or skin lesions, see a dermatologist promptly.

Afamelanotide: The Approved Exception

Afamelanotide (brand name SCENESSE) is an FDA-approved melanocortin medication — but not for cosmetic tanning. It is approved to increase pain-free light exposure in adults with erythropoietic protoporphyria (EPP), a rare inherited disorder that causes severe phototoxic pain, and it is administered by specialist clinicians as an implant.[2]

In other words, the one melanocortin peptide with regulatory approval is a specialist treatment for a rare disease — not a shortcut to a summer tan.
